Wood Meadow-Mouse

[Plate XII.b]

  • Arvicola scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841][1]

Description.—Head and body of the adult male, in November, nearly 3½ inches long; tail, nearly ¾ of an inch; hind-foot, ⅝ of an inch. Head, large; snout, blunt; ears, concealed by the fur; eyes, very small; fur on the back, soft, glossy and smooth; hairs, plumbeous at the base, broadly tipped with dark-brown on the back, and slightly tipped with white and reddish-brown on the belly. Young, color like that of the adult. This species is readily distinguished from the other arvicolæ found in this vicinity, by its smaller size, glossy fur, large muzzle, small eyes, and very short tail. It is common in the woods in Northern Illinois and Southern Wisconsin, where it is less abundant, however, than the two preceding. This is probably the species mentioned under the name of Arvicola riparius, by Dr. Plummer, in Silliman’s Journal, as existing in Wayne county, Indiana.

Unlike the two species last described, this, so far as I have observed, lives exclusively in the woods, or in fields surrounded by timber, though it becomes more numerous in the clearing and prefers high ground. Its home, as noticed here, is under the leaves, and its mode of life brings strongly to mind the Sorex dekayi[2]

Pipfrosch Press Taxonomy Notes

Note: This section does not appear in the original article.

The author neglected to specify an authority for the binomial name Arvicola scalopsoides.

Arvicola scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841] is now considered to be a junior synonym of Microtus pinetorum [Le Conte, 1830]. Several subspecies are described, Illinois falls within the range of the subspecies M. p. scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841] although many no longer recognize the validity of any subspecies within M. pinetorum.

This species is currently referred to using the common name ‘Woodland Vole’.

[1] The author did not specify the authority for Arvicola scalopsoides. The correct authority of [Audubon and Bachman, 1841] has been added.

[2] This may be a reference to Arvicola dekayi [Audubon and Bachman, 1854; TSN 971204] which is now a junior synonym for Microtus pennsylvanicus [Ord, 1815; TSN 180297]. However, the genus Sorex [Linnæus, 1758] is usually used for shrews, and both DeKay as well as Audobon and Bachman referenced a shrew with the name ‘DeKay’s Shrew’ (Sorex dekayi). My suspicion is that Sorex dekayi was first moved into the genus Arvicola and is in fact now a junior synonym of Microtus pennsylvanicus [Ord, 1815].

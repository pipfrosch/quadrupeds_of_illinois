Wood Meadow-Mouse

[Plate XII.b]

  • Arvicola scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841]

Description.—Head and body of the adult male, in November, nearly 3½ inches long; tail, nearly ¾ of an inch; hind-foot, ⅝ of an inch. Head, large; snout, blunt; ears, concealed by the fur; eyes, very small; fur on back, soft, glossy, and smooth; hairs, plumbeous at the base, broadly tipped with dark-brown on the back, and slightly tipped with white and reddish-brown on the belly. Young, color like that of the adult. This species is readily distinguished from the other arvicolæ found in this vicinity, by its smaller size, glossy fur, large muzzle, small eyes, and very short tail. It is common in the woods of in Northern Illinois and Southern Wisconsin, where it is less abundant, however, than the two preceding. This is probably the species mentioned under the name of Arvicola riparius, by Dr. Plummer, in Silliman’s Journal, as existing in Wayne county, Indiana.

Pipfrosch Press Taxonomy Notes

Note: This section does not appear in the original article.

The author neglected to specify an authority for the binomial name Arvicola scalopsoides.

Given both the specific name and the description the author gives, he is almost certainly referencing Arvicola scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841; TSN 971223] which is now considered to be a junior synonym of Microtus pinetorum [Le Conte, 1830]. Several subspecies are described, Illinois falls within the range of the subspecies M. p. scalopsoides [Audubon and Bachman, 1841] although many no longer recognize the validity of any subspecies within M. pinetorum.

This species is currently referred to using the common name ‘Woodland Vole’.

Prairie-Dog

[Plate IX.b]

  • Spermophilus ludovicianus [Ord, 1815]

Description.—Length of adult male from nose to root of tail, 13 inches; tail, (vertebræ,) 2⅝ inches. Back, reddish-brown mixed with grey and black; belly, soiled white; tail, banded with brown near the tip.

The famous “Prairie Dog,” or “Prairie Squirrel,” exists in great abundance on the plains west of the Missouri River, being also found over a large extent of country at the southward, and it has also been observed as far west as California. At the southward, Captain Marcy found it above Cross Timbers, on the Red River of Louisiana. Though not properly included among the mammals of this region, this squirrel has more resemblance to our grey spermophile in color, and to the wood-chuck (Arctomys monax) in form, than to any other known to our farmers. Its habits are not unlike those of our grey and striped spermophiles. Like them, it is strictly a prairie animal, subsisting upon plants, and probably on some insects.

The prairie dogs are fond of each other’s company, and live together, in great congregations, their numerous burrows, situated close together, being called “prairie-dog towns.” The burrow is much deeper and more extensive than that of either of our two Illinois prairie-squirrels. Like these, it is fond of standing erect near its hole, ventures from it with caution, and hastens into it when alarmed; but, as if it were inquisitive, soon puts out its head again to look at the cause of its fright. Like the rest of the genus, it hibernates. Its note is said to have gained it the name of “Prairie Dog,” but its voice can scarcely bear much resemblance to the bark of a dog, and is probably more like the whistling chatter of our grey and striped spermophiles, though perhaps less musical and clear.

Numbers of our common [Sic] prairie rattlesnakes (Crotalophorus tergeminus)[1], as well as of a small owl, live habitually in the burrows of the prairie-dog. These can hardly be welcome guests of the rightful inhabitants. Though the owl probably feeds chiefly upon insects, it might devour the young; and the rattlesnake certainly does, as well as makes many a meal of the adults.

Pipfrosch Press Taxonomy Notes

Note: This section does not appear in the original article.

The binomial nomenclature of this species is an excellent example of the rules of taxonomy nomenclature in action.

The first description of this species that gave it a binomial name came in 1815 by Ord, giving it the name Arctomys ludovicianus placing it in the existing genus Arctomys [Schreber, 1780; TSN 930222].

A second description of this species giving it a binomial name came in 1817 by Rafinesque—apparently unaware Ord had already described it—giving it the name Cynomys socialis. Rafinesque did not believe this species fit any existing genera, so he defined the genus Cynomys using Cynomys socialis as the type species for the genus.

As the Cynomys socialis of Rafinesque is the same species as Arctomys ludovicianus of Ord, Cynomys socialis is an invalid junior synonym because Arctomys ludovicianus was described first.

Kennicott, the author of this paper (1857), recognized that this species did not belong in the genus Arctomys. He placed it in the Ground Squirrel genus Spermophilus [F. Cuvier, 1825; TSN 180145]. I do not know if Kennicott was the first to use Spermophilus ludovicianus or if he followed another naturalist, that binomial combination does not seem to have been used very often.

Just a year later in 1857, in 1858, Baird also recognized that this species did not belong in Arctomys. Baird however did not feel this species belonged in Spermophilus either, or any other genera currently in use. As Rafinesque was the first to recognize this species belonged in a novel genus, even though the binomial name Cynomys socialis is an invalid junior synonym to Arctomys ludovicianus, the rules of taxonomy nomenclature give priority to Cynomys for the genus name since Rafinesque was the first to recognize that this species needed a novel genus. Baird thus correctly used the name Cynomys ludovicianus, combining the specific name from the original description by Ord with the genus name by Rafinesque.

As Cynomys ludovicianus is now the binomial nomenclature for the type species to the genus Cynomys, it is highly unlikely the accepted binomial nomenclature for this species will ever change again. The only ways that could happen:

  1. Cynomys ludovicianus is shown to be a junior synonym for an earlier described species. Not going to happen, this species is clearly distinct from all earlier described species.
  2. Cladistics show that Cynomys ludovicianus aligns closely with a previously described genus such that placing this species in the previously described genus would solve a paraphyletic clade issue. Also highly unlikely to ever happen.

Unless for some reason we are compelled to abandom binomial nomenclature, the species described by Ord will almost certainly always be known as Cynomys ludovicianus [Ord, 1815].

This species is currently referred to using the common name ‘Black-tailed Prairie Dog’.

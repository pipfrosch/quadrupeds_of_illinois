Muskrat

[Plate XIV.a, Photo 20]

  • Fiber zibethicus [Linnæus, 1766]

Description.—A full-grown muskrat is about 15 inches in length, from nose to root of tail, and the tail is 10 inches. The body is heavy, eyes small, ears concealed, and incisor teeth large; the tail large, nearly naked, and flattened laterally; legs, short, hind-feet, with the toes flattened and partly palmated; beneath the long hair is a coat of dense, soft and very warm fur; color of the body, ashy-brown above, ashy on the underparts.

The muskrat, or “Musquash,” abounds in the greater part of North America, though not in certain of the Southeastern States. It is found northward to the Polar Sea. It is strictly aquatic, its stout tail and muscular hind-legs, being provided with broad feet and toes, furnish it efficient means of locomotion in the water, while its thick, downy fur protects it from the wet. On land, its movements are as awkward as those of a duck. It naturally seeks its food in the water, away from which it never takes up its abode.

Pipfrosch Press Taxonomy Notes

Note: This section does not appear in the original article.

This species was originally described as Castor zibethicus [Linnæus, 1766]. The genus Fiber [Dumeril. 1805; TSN 900101] used by the author is now considered to be a homonyn and junior synonym of Castor. However, the species is now the type species for the monotypic genus Ondatra [Link, 1795; TSN 180317] resulting in the binomial name Ondatra zibethicus [Linnæus, 1766] as the currently used binomial nomenclature. Numerous subspecies have been described but many do not currently accept them as valid.
